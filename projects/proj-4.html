Overview

This was a small university project I was involved in that aimed to improve the testing capabilities of the on-campus wind tunnel. The objective was to create a miniature electric propulsion thrust stand that was able to measure the thrust, RPM, and airspeed produced by a 9-inch propeller on a brushless DC motor. The electrical telemetry of the system, such as the voltage and current, had to be capable of being monitored in real-time. Additionally, the system required a hardware emergency-stop button.

I was assigned to be Project Manager of a small team of 6, as well as the motor subsystem designer. Most of the initial work was doing electrical analysis to determine what kind of parts we needed to order for the stand. This involved power calculations to determine what kind of motor would be sufficient to produce a required thrust with a specifically sized propeller, what different sensors would be needed to perform the data measurements, and what kind of battery would be needed to provide power to all these systems, as well as would they be electrically compatible with one another and be able to interface through physical connections. Various calculations were done by the team as well as KiCAD schematics created to justify the electrical interfaces of each of the subsystems individually and connected together. I performed power calculations, conducted research, and created KiCAD electrical schematics for the brushless DC motor, as well as the system in full. After our bill of materials and electrical schematics were approved by our advisors, I ordered the parts on commercially available sites, mostly from Amazon.

After the parts arrived, the team worked on building their respective subsystems, as well as a Graphic User Interface (GUI) to control the thrust stand and display telemetry data. I programmed an Arduino UNO R3 microcontroller using Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) control architecture to dictate the RPM of the motor based on user specification. This means that the user is able to input a value to determine the speed at which the propeller spins, and is able to adjust that value while it's spinning. I collaborated with my team closely on this to ensure that each of our individual programs don't interfere with one another, and are able to interface seamlessly with the GUI.

I also had responsibility conducting progress checks on my members, documenting calculations, organizing supplies, and ensuring that our design work corroborates with the project requirements. I was also the main point of contact to the advisors of the project on our progress, and wind tunnel personnel to determine how our prototype could get integrated into the wind tunnel.

At the end of the project, although not very visually flattering, we were able to create a fully functional electric motor thrust stand and GUI with a user controlled motor. It could accurately measure thrust that the motor and propeller were producing using a load cell to measure bending stress and converting that into thrust, it could measure RPM using an infrared sensor located just underneath the motor, and it could measure the air speed produced by the propeller and motor using a pressure sensor computing the difference between static pressure of the room and the dynamic pressure caused by the freestream. This data was also able to be displayed and dynamically plotted on the GUI as the sensors were gathering these measurements in real-time. We also ended up with a fully functional emergency-stop button that cut power from the battery to the motor once pressed. Our model was then handed off to the wind tunnel lab for further prototyping and integration.
