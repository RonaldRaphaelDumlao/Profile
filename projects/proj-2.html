Overview

Small Satellite Stationkeeping (S-Kubed) was an additional senior project I did for my last year at Cal Poly Pomona. It was a NASA JPL and USAF funded project with the intention of developing a Helmholtz Cage testbed for small satellites, as well as conduct field tests and document research on its performance. A Helmholtz Cage is a structure that is capable of generating a contained magnetic field, using a set of three orthogonal copper coils running along all three axes. The purpose is to cancel out Earth's ambient magnetic field, and simulate the magnetic environment of space, and in our case, Low-Earth Orbit (LEO). This would allow the hardware components of the 3U CubeSat from CubeSTEP (see CubeSTEP section in Projects) to be tested in the environmental conditions it must operate in.

By the time I joined the project, the Helmholtz Cage had already been built, however little testing had been performed on it, and the testing that did get performed yielded issues with the circuitry of the cage overheating, an uneven structural frame, and a non-uniform and weak magnetic field. I was assigned to Hardware Testing on the cage itself, where I troubleshot existing issues, conducted performance tests on its capabilities, and tested the cage using physical flight hardware.

Initially, I worked on fixing the cage's aluminum structure. The frame is what controlled the orientation of the copper coils, and some parts of the frame were mis-aligned and felt dangerously loose. I managed to easily re-align the frame by adjusting some screws and bolts near the base of the structure, as well as some loose copper wire in different areas. I also designed and 3D printed some basic brackets and connectors to stiffen the corners and connecting junctions of the frame so that the cage was more structurally sturdy.

I then transitioned to performing diagnostic tests on the cage to ensure its electronics are stable while it's running, as well as to analyze the quality of the magnetic field it generates. The team was able to build new circuits and a cooling system to prevent the electronics from overheating, however the magnetic field was still weak and distorted in some areas as read by a magnetometer, and it took us nearly the whole academic year to figure out that the non-uniformity was caused by the bolts and connectors around the frame being made of steel, which is a ferrous material. Steel is a magnetic material, so the outer areas of the magnetic field were getting absorbed and deflected by the steel around it. I, along with a couple of other members, were tasked with completely disassembling the cage, replacing all the steel connectors with brass ones, and reassembling it. This took many nights of careful tooling, execution, and organization since there were hundreds of steel connectors that needed replacing, and we needed to make sure that each of the coils were reassembled into their specific orientations.

The bulk of my work on the project came with designing, 3D printing, and building a simple mock model of the CubeSTEP Cerberus CubeSat, that mimicked its mass and inertia properties. The purpose of this was to conduct hardware testing on the satellite's flight hardware on a similar frame, while the actual satellite is yet to be fully assembled. I used Siemens NX to design the model, and the existing mass and inertia properties of the satellite for reference. I created a system of small weighted cubes that could be oriented in a specific way inside of the 3U frame to match the properties of the actual satellite. The mass properties were simply measured using a scale, while the inertia properties were measured using a trifilar pendulum to determine the exact mass moment of inertia values on all three axes. The mass came with a 5% error, while the inertias came with a cumulative error of 9%, but for a basic model, this was considered fine to use as a prototype.

Additionally, I designed a hardware testing platform meant to go atop an air-bearing stand, where the CubeSat could be safely mounted, as well as rotate freely and tilt to some degree. With the movable mount, I also designed a counter-weight system that allowed the system to have a neutral torque, meaning that if the CubeSat and mount were to tilt, they would stay in that position rather than restoring back to normal standing position. Both of these parts had to be manufactured out of stainless steel, so I created drawings of each of the parts using GD&T standards, specifying geometric tolerances in flatness, surface profile, position, and circular runout. To go along with this, I created a quality check list using AS9100 guidelines, and worked closely with the on-campus machinists and technicians to create these parts. I assisted in the manufacturing and assembly operations, working directly with CNC mills, lathes, and drills. After the parts were manufactured, I created First Article Inspection Reports to comply with AS9102 standards, ensuring that the parts met the GD&T requirements I specified. This was done through quality inspection using a Coordinate Measuring Machine (CMM) to accurately measure the parts' dimensions, angles, and tolerances, then comparing them against design requirements.

Since I was concurrently the Responsible Engineer for the ADCS subsystem on the CubeSTEP team, I was able to write procedures to test the sun sensors and magnetorquer inside of the Helmholtz cage using the testing hardware I designed. These testing procedures helped verify functionality and flight readiness of the ADCS subsystem.

Another outcome of being involved with both the S-Kubed and CubeSTEP teams was acting as the Liaison between them. On top of technical responsibilities, I also set up and led meetings between different teams and facilitated the exchange of important information, progress, and requests.

When I graduated, the project had a completely functional Helmholtz Cage with operational hardware and software, a mock CubeSat to use for further flight hardware testing, as well as a fully manufactured testing apparatus that was compatible for use inside the cage itself.
