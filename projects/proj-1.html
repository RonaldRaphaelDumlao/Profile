Overview

CubeSTEP was a senior project that I did for my last year at Cal Poly Pomona. It was a NASA JPL funded project that aimed to develop and launch a 3U Cube Satellite (CubeSat), called Cerberus, into Low-Earth Orbit (LEO) to document research on 3 different oscillating heat pipes (OHP). This was meant to increase the technology readiness of OHPs to justify their use on larger spacecraft in LEO. This project started in 2022 with initial multi-million dollar funding from NASA JPL, and when I graduated in May 2026, the project was nearing full satellite assembly and integration, and hoping to be ready for launch later in the year.

I joined the project after the team had just conducted their Critical Design Review (CDR) with NASA JPL, and were approved to proceed to final design manufacturing and integration. I took on the role of Responsible Engineer for the Attitude Determination & Control System (ADCS) and the Electrical Power System (EPS) on the project's Flight Hardware team. At this point, all the design work was complete and the parts ordered. My job was to be the point of contact for both the ADCS and EPS, and complete their assembly, integration, and testing (AIT) so that they may be flight ready for full satellite integration.

The ADCS hardware was composed of a magnetorquer and sun sensors, while the EPS hardware was composed of a battery pack, power distribution module (PDM), and solar arrays. My initial responsibilities were to conduct documentation of each subsystem's capabilities, interfaces, and testability. I authored 2 Interface Control Documents (ICD) for both the subsystems, which defined how they communicated and exchanged data with the satellite's On-Board Computer (OBC). These documents established the exact boundaries, protocols, formats, and physical connections required for integration with their software. This made it easy for the Flight Software team to build their command and telemetry program.

The bulk of my work on this project was facilitating AIT of the ADCS and EPS. Most of the hardware components, which were all ordered commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS) components, required bench validation to be performed on each one to confirm nominal functionality (i.e. do the components turn on, respond to stimuli, etc). I authored validation testing procedures for the sun sensors, battery pack, PDM, and solar arrays, which were written in compliance with NASA GEVS, a foundational aerospace standard to determine if flight hardware meets minimum testing requirements for safe use on spacecraft. I then conducted these procedures myself in a cleanroom setting in accordance with the NASA SSRI guidelines for cleanroom safety and recommended precations & protocols. I was able to successfully sign off on the bench validation of all ADCS and EPS hardware, which allowed the team to proceed with subsystem integration.

Towards the end of my time with the project, I was able to work with FlatSat testbed integration, as well as Hardware-In-The-Loop (HITL) testing. FlatSat testbeds are essentially engineering models where components of the satellite subsystems are laid out flat on a workbench, rather than in their final stacked form inside of the satellite structure. HITL tests are performed in order to observe how the hardware reacts to commands from connected software, as well as how they react to external stimuli and if they can send accurate telemetry/data back to the software user interface due to that external stimuli. Various tests were conducted this way, such as thermal sensitivity tests, light detection tests on the sun sensors, power output tests for the battery and PDM, sunlight power draw tests for the solar arrays, and many tests to determine how the hardware reacted to specific user commands. From these, the team recorded data and compared them with what was typical for these systems, allowed us to troubleshoot anomalous behavior, and adjust the software and re-calibrate the hardware accordingly.

By the time I graduated, I was able to successfully sign off and approve of the complete flight readiness of the ADCS and EPS subsystems, making them eligible for full assembly and integration with the complete satellite system.
