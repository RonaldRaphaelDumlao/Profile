Overview

UMRBA Propulsion was a university project team I got involved with earlier into my undergraduate career. Cal Poly Pomona has many different rocketry teams developing different kinds of solid and liquid propulsion rockets for competition and research. UMBRA Propulsion, while not a project working directly in the development of a certain rocket, was a project that designed, manufactured, and tested the propulsion systems of the different rocketry teams on campus.

I worked on the design and structural analysis of solid rocket motors and propellant. Over the course of the academic year I was involved with the project, I was assigned to work on 2 different motor and solid propellant commissions. The procedures I undertook for both were basically identical. I would initially have a meeting with the rocketry team's project manager to go over what the rocket was meant to be capable of, any preliminary designs they had for the motor, and any logistics that could help me out with making their motor. I would then take the preliminary CAD of their motor concept and review it, re-design some features to make it easily manufacturable, and design the internal insulation and solid propellant grain in accordance with the rocketry team's specifications. I ran burn simulations using OpenMotor and BurnSim softwares to analyze hypothetical thrust over time profiles based on the geometry of the motor and specified propellant grain. Based on the simulations, I would optimize the efficiency of the motor's thrust as much as I could, by adjusting the grain geometry and nozzle dimensions.

Once simulations were optimized, I would conduct basic structural analysis on the motor casing to ensure that the structure would maintain itself during burn. I ran thermal and vibration loading simulations on Ansys to determine how the nozzle and surrounding motor frame reacted under the extreme heat and mechanical noise of a rocket burn. Additionally, I conducted Finite Element Analysis using FMAP to stress test the motor structure under its own mass at different orientations.

After the rocketry teams approved of the motor, I would commission the motor parts and propellant grain to be manufactured. I created prints of the motor parts using GD&T standards calling out specified dimensional tolerances, as well as feature flatness, surface profiles, circularity, position, and surface finish specifications. In addition, I created custom quality inspection documents based on AS9100 guidelines and First Article Inspection (FAI) documents based on AS9102 guidelines. I was required to create these documents to submit along with the part drawings to ensure strict compliance with our own dictated inspection criteria, which go along with the manufacturer's own quality inspection and FAI documents.

Both of these motors were successfully manufactured and given to the rocketry teams for full assembly. Both teams conducted static fire tests on the motors, and I was able to witness both motors firing successfully and ending up with minimal wear for further static fire tests, as well as eventual launch.
